Five Rules pertaining to Apostrophes

  1. Plurals

    Never use apostrophes in plurals
    • Cameras, not Camera's
    • DVDs, not DVD's

  2. Contraction

    Apostrophes also signify contraction of two words into one, this is mostly used in informal writing
    • "You are" can be written "You're" (Note that "your" is different, it is possessive; see rule 3)
    • "They are" can be written "They're"
    • "It is" can be written "It's"
    • "Who is" can be written "Who's" (Note that "whose" is different)
    • "Can not" can be written "Can't" (Note that "can not" as two words is rarely used when the single word "cannot" exists)
    • "She would have" can be written "She'd've"

  3. Possession

    Apostrophes signify possesion
    • Adam's DVDs

    Note1: "your" signifies possession by "you". e.g. Your DVDs
    Note2: "whose" is a request for possession: who owns it? e.g. Whose book is this?
    Note3: "whose" is also a relative pronoun, e.g. James is someone whose opinion I value

  4. Possessive form of It

    Because "it's" means "it is" we use no apostrophe to signify possession in the case of "it"
    • Its premise (the premise of It), not It's premise (It is premise)

  5. Remove double-"S" (optional)

    Where an apostrophe is used and results in "s's" this can be shortened to "s'"
    • "James's house" can be written "James' house"
